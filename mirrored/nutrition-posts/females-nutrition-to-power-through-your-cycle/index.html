Females: Nutrition to Power Through Your Cycle

Ladies – did you know that 90% of us have experienced some form of PMS (premenstrual syndrome)? It strikes in many forms, but a few common symptoms include cramping, fatigue, headaches, bloating, anxiety and depression. For some, it’s a minor annoyance. For others, it can be debilitating. Neither is ideal when trying to feel and perform your best, but there’s good news – there are nutrition strategies you can follow to still perform at your highest level at any time of the month.

We are going to get into some pretty specific strategies in this article, especially when it comes to the timing of macronutrients. Please, do not try these unless you are already eating a mostly whole food diet and have a healthy relationship with food. Once you have the basics down, then you can try incorporating some of these things. For an overview on the basics of nutrition, see PowerPoint: Nutrition for Developing Swim Athletes.

Everyone’s cycle is different, but the average female has a cycle lasting 28 days. The cycle is divided into two main phases (there are more, but for our sake we will focus on these), the follicular (days 1-14) and luteal (days 15-28) phases. Menstruation (or bleeding) signals the start of the follicular phase of the menstrual cycle. The hormones at work during each of these phases fluctuate, causing a variety of things to happen in the body that can affect swim performance.

First and foremost, female swimmers should make sure they are getting ENOUGH calories – both on and off their period. In a different article (Getting Good Calories) we discussed the implications of not getting enough calories, or Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S). I don’t really recommend calorie counting, except for a very short time. You can do this for a week or less to get a feel for how much you are currently eating and what needs to be adjusted. It is interesting to note that during the luteal phase, a recent study found that resting energy expenditure (REE) increased by 2.5-11.5%. This means that female athletes could potentially need more calories during the luteal phase of their cycle (days 15-28).

We’ve all heard that carbohydrates are important for swim performance, as they are your main source of energy. During the follicular phase, there is an increase in carbohydrate oxidation (the use of carbs for energy), which makes it even more important to eat enough carbs (especially pre-exercise). As a general guideline, older swimmers should consume at least 0.45g/lb carbohydrates leading up to exercise, and at least 0.34g/lb after. Focus on whole, minimally processed sources of carbs like whole grains, veggies and fruits first to keep PMS symptoms at bay.

Adequate fat intake plays a huge role in hormone regulation and the absorption of vitamins and minerals. About 20-35% of your calories should come from healthy fats like avocados, fish, nuts, and seeds. During the luteal phase of the cycle there is an increase in fat oxidation (more fat is broken down for energy) and thus an increased need for healthy sources of fat.

Muscle repair and maintenance cannot occur without adequate protein. Sometime in the middle of the luteal phase, protein oxidation peaks with rising levels of estrogen and progesterone. It may be beneficial to pay special attention to protein intake during this time, however it’s always a good idea to know how much you are eating daily. Older swimmers should aim for a minimum of 0.73g/lb protein each day. Whole food sources of protein like beef, chicken, and salmon contain key vitamins and minerals like Iron and Vitamin B12, which both help combat PMS fatigue (as well as anemia) and keep you performing at your best. Iron delivers oxygen to lungs and skeletal muscles during exercise, helping you swim faster for longer periods of time. Because of blood loss during menstruation, it’s vital to ensure you’re getting enough iron. Other sources of iron (besides meats and fish) include dark leafy greens, eggs, legumes, seeds, and whole grains. Vitamin B12 is involved in forming and maintaining healthy nerve cells and red blood cells, as well as DNA synthesis. Not getting enough can mean poor memory, fatigue/weakness, loss of appetite/weight loss, and depression. Vitamin B12 is only found in animal products (beef, fish, shellfish, dairy), so it’s important to supplement B12 if you do not include any of these in your diet.

In addition to Iron and B12, some other micronutrients worth mentioning are Vitamin B6, Magnesium, Calcium, and Vitamin D. Magnesium and B6, when consumed together, have been shown to reduce symptoms of PMS. Magnesium may raise serotonin levels, which can positively impact your mood and sleep. Vitamin B6 helps your body use serotonin, which can combat depression. Try incorporating brown rice, pumpkin seeds, or almonds to up your magnesium intake, and pistachios, turkey, chickpeas, or potatoes for Vitamin B6. Vitamin D helps the body absorb Calcium, which has also been shown to reduce symptoms of PMS. A few food sources of Vitamin D include egg yolks, salmon, meat, and fortified dairy and juice. Some Calcium-rich foods to try would be dairy products, chia seeds, spinach and kale.

Finally, it’s a good idea to be mindful of how much caffeine and ultra-processed foods you are consuming on your period (and all the time). Caffeine can negatively affect mood and sleep, and ultra-processed foods are typically very high in sodium (which in excess can cause bloating) and very low in nutrients. Rather than caffeine, try some chamomile tea, which may ease cramping and anxiety. Make sure you are staying hydrated as well with water and electrolyte drinks to prevent cramping and a host of other problems (see It’s All About Hydration).

Bottom line: eating enough, and enough of the right things is crucial both on and off your period. Depending on the phase of the cycle you are in, there are nutrition strategies you can try to ensure you’re performing at your highest level. Focus on whole, minimally processed foods most of the time. The specifics discussed in this article (especially carbohydrate/protein increases during certain phases) are only for athletes who are already eating a mostly whole-food diet and have a healthy relationship with food.

RECAP:

Days 1-14 (follicular phase of cycle; day 1 = onset of menstruation)

  • Pay special attention to carbohydrates, especially before exercise
  • Eat foods that are high in Iron and Vitamin B12 (especially animal protein)
  • Eat foods with Vitamin B6 & Magnesium together
  • Eat foods with Calcium & Vitamin D together
  • Try chamomile tea

Days 15-28 (luteal phase of cycle)

  • Can increase calories slightly
  • Can increase healthy fat intake slightly
  • Can increase protein mid-phase

All The Time

  • Eat Enough
  • Eat less-processed, whole foods most of the time
  • Eat a variety of nutrient-dense foods
  • Limit caffeine (100 mg/day or less for older athletes)
  • Stay hydrated
